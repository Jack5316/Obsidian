# Concept Digest

## Inverse problem (decision analogy)
The inverse problem, as applied to life decisions, is the practice of starting with a desired outcome (**b**) and reverse-engineering the sequence of choices or inputs (**x**) that would produce it, rather than the trivial "forward problem" of calculating outcomes from given inputs. This framing highlights why hard choices feel difficult: they mirror the mathematical pathologies of inverse problems, including non-existent solutions, infinitely many solutions (underdetermined systems), and ill-conditioning (small shifts in desired outcomes leading to completely different required inputs). The analogy draws directly from linear algebra, where the inverse problem is defined as finding **x** given **A** and **b = Ax**.

## Forward problem (decision analogy)
The forward problem, in contrast to the inverse problem, is the trivial, mechanical process of calculating an outcome (**b**) from a given system (**A**) and input (**x**) — the "what if I do this?" calculation. In life decisions, this is almost never the problem we actually face; we rarely start with a fixed set of choices and compute their outcome, instead working backwards from desired states. The analogy emphasizes that the "easy direction" in linear algebra is not the one that matters for meaningful life choices.
Appears in: [[inverse-problems-life-decisions]], [[Essay - inverse problems]]

## Ill-conditioned problem
An ill-conditioned inverse problem is one where small, subtle changes in the desired outcome (**b**) produce wildly different required inputs (**x**). In life decisions, this manifests as punishing imprecision in what we *actually* want: for example, mistaking "being a lawyer" for "feeling respected" can lead to a completely different set of life choices, only to realize we solved the wrong problem entirely. This mathematical property explains why clarifying core desires is so critical for decision-making.
Appears in: [[inverse-problems-life-decisions]], [[Essay - inverse problems]]

## Underdetermined system
An underdetermined system is an inverse problem with infinitely many valid solutions (**x**) that produce the same desired outcome (**b**). In life, this sounds like freedom but often leads to analysis paralysis: when multiple paths seem to lead to the same destination, it becomes difficult to pick one, as there is no clear "right" answer to compute. This is one of the core pathologies that makes inverse life decisions hard.
Appears in: [[inverse-problems-life-decisions]], [[Essay - inverse problems]]

## Regularization (decision strategy)
Regularization is a mathematical technique for making ill-posed inverse problems solvable by adding constraints that prioritize stable, simple solutions — most famously Tikhonov regularization, which balances closeness to the desired outcome with simplicity. For life decisions, this translates to adding practical constraints like a simplicity bias (favoring paths with fewer moving parts), a "do no harm" rule (avoiding choices that close off future options), and values checks (ensuring solutions align with core desires). These constraints do not make the problem easier, but they make it *actable*.
Appears in: [[inverse-problems-life-decisions]], [[Essay - inverse problems]]

## Simplicity bias (regularization heuristic)
A simplicity bias is a regularization heuristic for life decisions that favors paths with fewer moving parts, less complexity, and lower overhead when multiple solutions to an inverse problem exist. It is a direct application of Tikhonov regularization’s preference for simple solutions, helping to cut through analysis paralysis by prioritizing choices that are easier to execute and less prone to breaking from unforeseen events.
